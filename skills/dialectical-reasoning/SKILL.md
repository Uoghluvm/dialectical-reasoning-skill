# Dialectical Reasoning

## Overview

Use dialectical reasoning to turn flat debates into structured analysis of contradictions, motion, conditions, and synthesis. Explain not only what conflicts with what, but also which contradiction is primary at the current stage, what may change, and under what conditions a new quality may emerge.

## Working Mode

1. Define the object, scale, and timeframe.
   Distinguish whether the user wants concept learning, case analysis, comparison, critique, or strategic guidance. Refuse to treat a system-level contradiction as if it were only an individual preference clash.
2. Surface the contradiction set.
   Name the opposing forces, tendencies, interests, or institutional logics. Avoid forcing every issue into a fake two-sided frame if several contradictions interact at once.
3. Identify the principal contradiction and the principal aspect.
   Explain why this contradiction is principal now, what evidence supports the call, and what secondary contradictions still constrain the situation.
4. Trace motion instead of snapshots.
   Show how each side depends on, restrains, transforms, or uses the other. Look for feedback loops, accumulation effects, thresholds, and stage shifts.
5. Separate structural conditions from temporary appearances.
   Mark what is durable, what is contingent, what is reversible, and what would materially change the conclusion.
6. Describe likely transformation paths.
   Explain how quantitative accumulation may trigger qualitative change, and how a contradiction may be reconfigured instead of merely "solved".
7. Produce a synthesis instead of a slogan.
   Do not collapse the result into "both sides are right". A dialectical synthesis may preserve tension, reorder priorities, or create a new form that contains both sides at a higher level.

## Output Shape

Use this structure unless the user asks for another format:

1. Object and stage
2. Contradiction map
3. Principal contradiction
4. Principal aspect at the current stage
5. Motion, thresholds, and turning points
6. Conditions that would change the analysis
7. Dialectical synthesis or practical conclusion

## Guardrails

- Do not confuse dialectics with empty moderation or rhetorical symmetry.
- Do not present timeless conclusions when the stage and conditions are unspecified.
- Do not turn every contradiction into a moral battle between good and bad camps.
- Do not ignore institutional, historical, or material constraints.
- Distinguish clearly between sourced facts, interpretive inference, and normative judgment.
- If the topic is political, historical, or ideological, separate descriptive analysis from endorsement.
